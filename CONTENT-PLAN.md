# EnerForge — First content batch (8 pieces)

Goal: build credibility as an independent project-intelligence operator by sharing a point of view
and a real worked case — **not** by selling services. Voice: personal, technical, grounded,
reflective, not salesy. Each piece ends with a soft CTA (read the case / follow along), never a
hard pitch.

All case numbers below come from the published case study and can be quoted as *illustrative*.

---

## 1. Customer savings are not enough for project finance

- **Core point:** A project can save the customer money and still fail the lender test. Savings and
  bankability are different questions.
- **Platform:** LinkedIn (lead post), blog (long form).
- **Structure:** Hook (a project with real savings that couldn't be financed) → the two questions
  most people merge → why a bank ignores "savings" → what it underwrites instead → takeaway.
- **Case insight to quote:** $2.08M/yr customer benefit, yet S1 minimum DSCR 0.60x vs 1.30x target.
- **CTA:** "I wrote up the full case — link in comments."

## 2. Avoided cost is not SPV revenue

- **Core point:** The customer's avoided cost and the project company's revenue are two different
  numbers. Only the contracted fee is bankable.
- **Platform:** LinkedIn, 小红书 (simple diagram: Avoided cost → EaaS fee → SPV revenue).
- **Structure:** Define avoided cost → define SPV revenue → the gap (capture rate) → why lenders
  only credit the contracted slice.
- **Case insight:** EaaS fee $1.31M/yr captures ~70% of the $2.08M/yr avoided benefit.
- **CTA:** "This is lens #2 of how I read a project — the rest are on the site."

## 3. Why a fixed EaaS fee beats BTM arbitrage

- **Core point:** Contracted revenue carries debt; market arbitrage is a bonus, not a base case.
- **Platform:** LinkedIn, blog.
- **Structure:** Three revenue stories (S1/S2/S3) → why only S1 is honest → what happens when you
  finance against upside → discipline over optimism.
- **Case insight:** S2 (with arbitrage) clears at 1.28x DSCR; S1 (fixed only) is 0.60x — the bank
  sees S1.
- **CTA:** "Full S1/S2/S3 breakdown in the case study."

## 4. Carbon value should be visible — but not double-counted

- **Core point:** Carbon benefit is real and worth showing, but parking it in base project revenue
  flatters the model and misleads a lender.
- **Platform:** LinkedIn, 小红书.
- **Structure:** Why carbon gets double-counted → who actually owns the benefit → how I present it
  (customer-side, separate line) → the integrity argument.
- **Case insight:** $67K/yr carbon value shown customer-side, deliberately excluded from SPV base
  revenue.
- **CTA:** "How I keep carbon honest in a model — short note on the blog."

## 5. A project can create value and still need financing optimisation

- **Core point:** "Not bankable" is not "no value." It usually means the structure needs work, not
  that the idea is dead.
- **Platform:** LinkedIn (reflective), blog.
- **Structure:** The trap of binary thinking (good/dead) → the real diagnosis (a single tail-year
  breaks coverage) → optimise vs abandon → what changes next.
- **Case insight:** S1 holds ~1.30x for Years 2–12; a single Year-13 sculpted repayment drops it to
  0.60x and sets the minimum.
- **CTA:** "I broke down exactly where it fails — case link."

## 6. How I read a weak-grid industrial BESS project

- **Core point:** A walkthrough of the five-lens method on a real weak-grid, diesel-exposed site.
- **Platform:** Blog (long form), LinkedIn (carousel of the 5 lenses).
- **Structure:** The site (weak grid, diesel, 4.8× peak-to-average) → each lens in turn → where the
  lenses interact → the verdict.
- **Case insight:** System sized to the steady load (PV 3.0 MWp, BESS 1.5 MW / 6.0 MWh), diesel kept
  for peaks; 989,000 L/yr displaced.
- **CTA:** "The five lenses live on the site under Project Lens."

## 7. Early feasibility should decide: proceed, optimise, redesign or stop

- **Core point:** The job of early feasibility is a clear decision, not a perfect forecast.
- **Platform:** LinkedIn, blog.
- **Structure:** What early-stage analysis is for → the four honest outcomes → why "stop" is a
  valid, valuable result → the cost of deciding late.
- **Case insight:** This case's outcome was "optimise" — proceed to a controlled development gate
  (EPC quote, EaaS term sheet, debt restructure), not a final investment decision.
- **CTA:** "This is the whole reason I built EnerForge — about page."

## 8. What I learned building EnerForge

- **Core point:** A personal, reflective piece on connecting technical, commercial, carbon and
  financing logic that's usually assessed in silos.
- **Platform:** LinkedIn (founder voice), blog.
- **Structure:** Where I started (climate / emissions modelling) → the silo problem I kept hitting →
  what I built and why → what I still find hard → an open invitation to compare notes.
- **Case insight:** Reference the case as the first worked example of the method end-to-end.
- **CTA:** "If you work on industrial energy projects, I'd genuinely like to compare notes."

### Sequencing suggestion

Lead with **#1** (the strongest, most counter-intuitive hook), then **#2** and **#3** to establish
the core mental model, **#4–#5** to show judgement, **#6–#7** to show method, and close the first
batch with **#8** as the personal/credibility piece. Roughly one per week keeps a steady, non-spammy
cadence. Reuse the case study as the anchor link across all eight.
