Project note 11 Market view Long read

How many different energy storage markets are there?

Reflections after studying the Australian market — where I learned that “energy storage” is not one business, but several, each with different customers, revenue models and financing structures.

When I first started looking into the energy storage industry, I assumed there was only one business model: selling batteries.

The deeper I went, the more I realised that energy storage has evolved into several completely different markets. While batteries are the common technology underpinning all of them, the customers, revenue models, financing structures and competitive dynamics are often entirely different.

01

Residential battery storage

Most mature

Today, the most mature segment in Australia remains residential battery storage. Growth is being driven by rising electricity prices, widespread rooftop solar adoption and government incentives. The business model is relatively straightforward, the customer base is enormous and demand growth is highly predictable. At the same time, it is also one of the most competitive markets, where products are increasingly standardised and success often comes down to distribution, installation capability and pricing.

02

Commercial & industrial (C&I) storage

Cost & reliability

C&I storage serves factories, warehouses, shopping centres and commercial facilities. Compared with residential storage, projects are larger and customers are more focused on reducing energy costs and improving reliability. As a result, developers need to understand load profiles, tariff structures and site-specific operating conditions. Although the market is growing rapidly, projects take longer to develop and require stronger technical and commercial capabilities.

03

Utility-scale battery storage

Capital & markets

Utility-scale battery storage has become one of the most attractive investment sectors in Australia’s energy transition. Many projects are measured in hundreds of megawatts, with capital costs running into hundreds of millions of dollars. In this market, the battery itself is often not the primary focus. Success depends on electricity market participation, revenue forecasting, project financing and long-term asset management. Unsurprisingly, the sector is dominated by large developers, energy companies and infrastructure funds with significant financial resources.

04

Industrial decarbonisation storage

Where I focus

This is perhaps the segment I find most interesting. Mining operations, industrial facilities, data centres and remote energy users are not simply purchasing batteries. They are seeking energy security, diesel displacement, emissions reduction and long-term operational resilience. Projects are highly customised, development cycles can be lengthy and risks are more complex. However, the value of individual projects is often substantially higher than traditional commercial storage opportunities. Unlike utility-scale storage, this market still contains many customers that remain underserved.

And then the emerging edges

Beyond these major segments, several emerging markets are gaining momentum across Australia:

Remote microgrids — solar + batteries + diesel for isolated communities and mines Virtual Power Plants — thousands of home batteries aggregated into a grid resource EV charging — increasingly integrated with on-site storage Data centre energy — reliable, low-carbon power for AI-driven demand

From a market-size perspective, residential batteries and utility-scale storage are currently the largest opportunities. From a future-growth perspective, Virtual Power Plants, data centre energy and industrial decarbonisation storage appear particularly promising. In terms of barriers to entry, utility-scale storage and VPPs require significant capital, market expertise and trading capability — while industrial decarbonisation, microgrids and selected C&I projects rely more heavily on project development and stakeholder coordination skills.

After studying these markets, one conclusion stands out. The energy storage sector may not be short of batteries, and it may not be short of installers. What it is likely to remain short of are people who can do something harder:

integrate customer needs, technical solutions, financing structures, contractual frameworks and project execution into a bankable project.

At first glance, everyone appears to be working in the same industry. In reality, some are selling equipment, some are selling energy savings, some are providing energy services, and others are managing the cash flows of infrastructure assets that will operate for decades.

That gap — between a battery and a bankable project — is the whole reason I’m building EnerForge around industrial decarbonisation storage.
