Project note 04 Utility-scale BESS · project finance · offtake structure

Capital is no longer the constraint on Australian battery financing

Notes from a conversation with Azure Capital’s Harrison Moore on Modo Energy’s “The Transmission” — where the lesson was that the NEM’s battery bottleneck has moved from money to grid connection timing and contract structure. Utility-scale is not the market Heliovulcan works in, but the financing logic reads directly onto industrial and weak-grid projects.

Harrison Moore is a partner at Azure Capital and has advised on more than 10 GW of battery transactions in Australia. For most of the last decade, the instinct with a battery project was to ask a single question: can we fund it? On his account of the market, that question has quietly flipped.

Debt raises last year were, in his words, “multiples oversubscribed” — there is more capital, debt and equity, chasing batteries than there are investable, shovel-ready projects to absorb it. If money is abundant, money is no longer where deals are won or lost. The binding constraints have moved upstream, to the grid connection queue and to the structure of the contract that sits on top of the asset.

Capital is abundant; shovel-ready projects are scarce

If capital is commoditised and ready-to-build projects are scarce, the value accrues to whoever can actually produce one — land secured, approvals in hand, connection locked, procurement lined up. Cheap capital is table stakes; development capability is the differentiator.

There is a second scarcity underneath the first. Even with capital abundant, the investors deploying it are throttled by attention. Moore describes funds with ten mandate-fit assets in front of them but the resources to seriously diligence only two or three at a time. Deals compete not only on merit but on how easy they are to underwrite — making the lender’s work easy is a financing strategy, not a soft skill.

The connection queue reprices the entire deal

Ask what actually gates a project to financial close and the answer keeps returning to the grid connection — a “log jam” of applications against constrained AEMO and network service provider processing capacity. The part worth internalising is that a connection date is not a milestone you passively wait on. It is a live variable that reprices everything downstream of it.

The battery supplier quotes its price against an assumed delivery date, and the offtaker prices its contract against the same date. When the date slips — as it routinely does — both negotiations reopen at once. The vendor loads in an uncertainty premium and capital cost rises; the offtaker discounts for the same uncertainty and revenue falls. A single physical and regulatory variable feeds simultaneously into the cost line and the revenue line, squeezing the contracted margin and thinning debt-service coverage.

If a financial model treats the connection date as a fixed input, it is hiding a two-sided repricing risk that lands on both CAPEX and revenue — and therefore directly on DSCR.

This is why a growing number of sponsors now commit to build before securing connection or offtake certainty, and solve the revenue side later. They are accepting more upfront risk in order to freeze the one variable — the timeline — whose drift was doing the most damage.

Contracting is a dial, not a switch

The old binary — a 100% physical toll or fully merchant — is gone. The market has moved through physical tolls to virtual tolls, which can be layered across multiple offtakers, and capacity swaps, which run on a joint operating protocol with a fixed-for-floating payment. The distinction matters more than the labels suggest: a capacity swap aligns dispatch through an agreed auto-bidding protocol, while a virtual toll can leave the operator dispatching differently from the instructions it is notionally receiving. That operational-control gap is real, and allocated differently deal by deal.

More importantly, contracting is best understood as the dial that sets the whole capital structure. A fully merchant project attracts an extra layer of lender conservatism — lower gearing, higher pricing, cash sweeps, shorter effective tenor. Add a contracted base and the lender has a floor that underwrites minimum debt service, which makes the merchant tail above it far easier to underwrite. The most elegant tool Moore describes is the gearing step-up: lenders pre-agree to release incremental debt if and when the sponsor secures further contracting, so the financing itself pulls offtake into existence. Gearing, pricing and tenor are functions of the contracted fraction — and the goal is the right fraction, not the maximum.

Why today’s lender appetite may be cyclical

Moore says lender appetite for merchant exposure has expanded faster than in any sector he has seen in sixteen years. It is tempting to read that as a permanent structural shift. But it is at least partly an artefact of the capital-opportunity imbalance: when more money competes for fewer deals, lenders are pushed up the merchant-risk learning curve to win mandates. Risk tolerance widened because capital is competing — not necessarily because the underlying risk became safer. If the imbalance reverses through overbuild or capital reallocation, conservatism can snap back. The disciplined posture is to lock in generous terms while the window is open, and stress-test the model against a reversion.

Two things that cut against the consensus

  • Government schemes are catalysts, not crutches. The Capacity Investment Scheme, LTESA and system-strength tenders are almost always paired with private offtake — Moore has not seen one underwrite a project standalone. They are most useful for seeding longer-duration (four-hour-plus) economics that are still marginal on merchant revenue alone.
  • Buy the solar everyone wrote off. His one contrarian conviction is operating solar, not batteries. Battery overbuild — the very risk that would compress storage margins — is what absorbs the negative pricing and curtailment currently hurting solar. A portfolio holding both is internally hedged.
  • Four-hour is becoming bankable unaided. Four-hour projects are starting to finance on private offtake without government support — earlier than the scheme-dependence narrative implies.

What the numbers actually said

  • Financing process: roughly six months end to end, of which two to four months is preparation before the market is approached, then about two months of credit approval.
  • Early engagement: sometimes 12–24 months before financial close, feeding bankability into the project contracts.
  • Project scale: from sub-50 MW early projects to a 200–400 MW current sweet spot, with the Waratah Super Battery at 850 MW.
  • Debt demand: raises described as “multiples oversubscribed”.
  • Caveat: no DSCR, gearing, tenor or CAPEX/MWh figures were disclosed — a notable gap for anyone trying to benchmark.
Battery financeability in the NEM is no longer about access to capital. It is about connection timing and contract structure — a physical variable and a legal one.

Why this reads onto industrial and weak-grid projects

Utility-scale NEM batteries are not the market Heliovulcan works in. But the most useful part of the conversation runs in reverse for industrial, weak-grid and EaaS projects. Those markets do not yet have the deep, educated capital pool that utility-scale batteries now enjoy — which means, on this kind of project, the developer is often the one educating a lender rather than choosing between competing term sheets.

The practical takeaways carry across directly. Treat connection and contract certainty as the things to lock down first, because they price everything else. Structure a contracted base of cash flow — the EaaS equivalent of Moore’s “contracted floor” — that carries the debt and compensates a lender for unfamiliarity. And model timeline slippage as a shock to both cost and revenue, not merely a later start date. That is where a project quietly becomes, or stops being, financeable.
